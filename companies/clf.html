Company Overview

📈 SLX (Steel ETF) — benchmark for Cleveland-Cliffs' steel market exposure

Cleveland-Cliffs is the US's largest flat-rolled steel producer and the country's largest iron ore pellet producer, formed through the combination of the former AK Steel, ArcelorMittal USA, and Stelco Canada. Unlike most US steelmakers who rely on scrap in electric arc furnaces, Cliffs operates blast furnaces fed by its own iron ore pellets from Minnesota and Michigan mines — a unique integrated model that provides cost stability but also capital intensity. The company supplies approximately 50% of the US automotive steel market, making it the dominant flat-rolled steel supplier to Ford, GM, and Stellantis.

Commodity Exposures

Steel Iron Ore Coal

Steel selling prices are the dominant earnings driver for Cleveland-Cliffs. The company's integrated model means iron ore pellet costs are largely internalized — Cliffs mines its own ore and pelletizes it for its blast furnaces, providing a cost advantage over mills that purchase pellets on the open market. Coking coal is a purchased input for blast furnace steelmaking. The company also produces hot-briquetted iron (HBI) at its Toledo, Ohio facility — a blast furnace alternative that can be charged into electric arc furnaces, bridging the gap between integrated and mini-mill steelmaking.

Price Sensitivity

Steel price movements are the primary earnings driver, with the hot-rolled coil (HRC) benchmark most relevant for CLF's flat-rolled product mix. US automotive steel demand is the key volume driver — slowing auto production directly impacts Cliffs' order book. The company's blast furnace model creates high fixed costs and significant operating leverage: when steel prices rise above breakeven, incremental revenue flows largely to EBITDA. Iron ore pellet prices are a secondary factor; natural gas costs matter for the DRI/HBI Toledo facility. Import competition and tariff policy are important structural factors for US steel pricing.
