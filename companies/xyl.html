Company Overview

📈 PHO (Water ETF) — benchmark for water technology sector performance

Xylem is the leading global water technology company, providing pumps, sensors, treatment systems, and smart water management infrastructure to utilities, industrial, commercial, and residential customers in over 150 countries. The company generates approximately $7 billion in annual revenue and became the dominant US water technology company following its 2023 acquisition of Evoqua Water Technologies. Xylem's product portfolio spans the entire water cycle — from intake through treatment, distribution, use, and wastewater — making it a pure-play on global water infrastructure spending.

Commodity Exposures

Water

Xylem is not a commodity producer but rather a solutions provider that benefits from water scarcity and infrastructure investment trends. As water scarcity intensifies globally — driven by climate change, population growth, and industrial demand — utilities and industries invest in more efficient water infrastructure, smart monitoring systems, and advanced treatment technologies. Xylem's digital water network monitoring and analytics business (AMI — Advanced Metering Infrastructure) is growing rapidly, driven by utility modernization programs. The Evoqua acquisition added industrial water treatment capabilities for pharmaceutical, power, and food & beverage sectors.

Price Sensitivity

Xylem's revenue is driven by water infrastructure spending cycles rather than water commodity prices directly. Key demand drivers include: (1) US infrastructure legislation (IIJA water funding), (2) European water directive compliance spending, (3) industrial facility capital expenditure, and (4) utility modernization programs. Water scarcity awareness accelerates all of these spending categories. The company's aftermarket services (replacement parts, maintenance contracts, digital subscriptions) provide recurring revenue that smooths the capital project cycle. Steel and copper are meaningful input costs for pumps and treatment equipment, making materials inflation a margin risk.
