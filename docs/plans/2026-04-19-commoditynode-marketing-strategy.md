## 1. Strategic Diagnosis

CommodityNode already has a strong product surface.
The biggest opportunity is not building more features first.
It is making the market understand exactly what kind of product this is.

Right now, the site is strongest when a visitor already understands that commodity moves affect equities and portfolios.
The marketing job is to make that value legible faster, more often, and to more people.

### The core problem to solve
Most visitors can understand:
- gold is up
- oil is down
- coffee crashed

But they still do not immediately know:
- which stocks / sectors matter
- whether the move is tactical or structural
- what action to take next
- why CommodityNode is better than just reading finance news + looking at charts

### Strategic conclusion
CommodityNode should not market itself as a commodity data site.
It should market itself as a commodity-to-position decision workflow.

Recommended master positioning:
- From raw commodity moves to stock and sector decisions.

Supporting positioning:
- See the commodity shock before the street reprices.
- Translate commodity headlines into equity decisions.
- Know who wins, who loses, and what to watch next.

## 2. Ideal Customer Profiles (using product-marketing-context)

### ICP 1: Self-directed investors
Pain:
- read commodity headlines but cannot quickly map them to their holdings

What they want:
- a fast answer to “what does this mean for the names I own or watch?”

Why they buy:
- saves time
- gives confidence
- feels closer to institutional workflow without institutional tooling cost

### ICP 2: Macro / event-driven traders
Pain:
- need scenario framing before OPEC / Fed / WASDE / geopolitical headlines fully propagate

What they want:
- a fast pre-event and post-event translation layer

Why they buy:
- simulator + forecast + downstream winners/losers framing

### ICP 3: Equity analysts / sector analysts
Pain:
- commodity sensitivity analysis is manual, repetitive, and slow

What they want:
- a reusable workflow for margin / earnings / supply-chain exposure analysis

Why they buy:
- faster note prep
- better second-order framing

### ICP 4: PM / research desks
Pain:
- hidden commodity exposure across many names

What they want:
- shared, repeatable, team-usable market intelligence workflow

Why they buy:
- better monitoring coverage and faster cross-asset translation

## 3. Funnel Strategy (using marketing-ideas + page-cro)

### Top of funnel: attract
Goal:
- capture people searching for or reacting to live commodity moves

Core assets:
- hot signal reports
- commodity hub pages
- theme pages
- company pages
- simulator / pricing pages for SEO and comparison intent

Winning traffic themes:
- why [commodity] up today
- [commodity] price impact on stocks
- [company] sensitivity to [commodity]
- [commodity] forecast 2026
- [event] impact on [commodity]

### Middle of funnel: educate + translate
Goal:
- move visitor from “interesting move” to “I need this workflow”

Core path:
- report → commodity hub → forecast → simulator → pricing

Critical requirement:
- every content page must point to the next action in the workflow, not just more reading

### Bottom of funnel: convert
Goal:
- make Pro feel like the obvious next step once someone understands the workflow

Conversion trigger:
- the moment a visitor realizes they need stock-level, scenario-level, or forward-looking context instead of only narrative

Meaning:
- pricing must sell outcomes, not features
- report / hub CTAs must point to the paid unlock as a decision advantage, not a paywall annoyance

## 4. Messaging Architecture (using copywriting)

Every major page should speak in this order:

### Layer 1: market situation
What moved?
What changed?
Why now?

### Layer 2: translation
Who wins?
Who loses?
Which sectors / names / supply chains matter?

### Layer 3: next action
What should the visitor do now?
- open the live hub
- check the forecast
- run the scenario
- unlock stock sensitivity

This sequence is more important than any specific headline tweak.

## 6. Page-Level CRO Strategy (using page-cro)

### Homepage
What is working:
- product feels real and live
- category breadth is visible
- workflow framing already exists

What to change next:
1. Replace generic “latest reports” framing with “Today’s actionable setups”
2. Add a 3-card block directly below the hero showing today’s best setups
3. Each setup should contain:
   - move
   - immediate implication
   - one CTA

Recommended homepage block title:
- Today’s actionable setups

Card structure:
- Commodity + move
- One-line implication
- CTA: “Open the live setup”

### Reports archive
What is working:
- strong freshness signal
- useful sorting and filtering

What to change next:
1. Add “Today’s market regime” strip above the list
2. Surface “best current setup” or “editor’s pick” card
3. Make archive feel like a decision shelf, not a chronology shelf

Recommended archive framing:
- Best current setup
- Highest disagreement
- Best pre-event read

### Pricing page
What is working:
- already speaks to real users and workflows

What to change next:
1. Reframe plan comparison by outcome, not features
2. Clarify the difference between Free and Pro as:
   - Free = observe the move
   - Pro = act before repricing
3. Add one specific “what you miss without Pro” comparison tied to real workflow moments

Recommended copy framing:
- Free helps you understand the move.
- Pro helps you decide what to do with it.

### Simulator page
What is working:
- strong product differentiation
- clear value to event-driven users

What to change next:
1. Add more pre-event framing
2. Add embedded examples linked to current market setups
3. Use current live examples from today’s hot movers, not only evergreen oil examples

## 7. Channel Strategy (using marketing-ideas)

### Priority 1: SEO
Why:
- site already has many indexable landing surfaces
- content naturally matches high-intent search behavior

Focus clusters:
1. Why up/down today pages
2. Commodity-to-stock pages
3. Forecast pages
4. Event-driven pages
5. Competitor comparison pages for adjacent search intent

### Priority 2: Social / short-form distribution
Use each hot report as a daily social asset.

Post format:
- move
- implication
- who wins / loses
- forecast angle
- link to report or hub

Good recurring structure:
- “Today’s commodity setup”
- “One move, three implications”
- “What the market is missing in [commodity] today”

### Priority 3: Newsletter
Two cadences:
1. Daily / near-daily “3 moves that matter”
2. Weekly “commodity regime briefing”

Purpose:
- retention
- repeated product education
- conversion from readers who are not ready to buy immediately

### Priority 4: Partnerships / audience borrowing
Best fits:
- macro newsletters
- commodity-focused creators
- finfluencers with sector-specific audiences
- Substack writers covering energy, mining, inflation, supply chains

Offer shape:
- sponsor / swap around a specific hot report or event playbook

## 8. Competitor Strategy (using competitor-alternatives)

CommodityNode should create comparison / alternative pages against adjacent tools and workflows.

Important note:
The goal is not to attack Bloomberg.
The real competitive comparison is often:
- finance news + TradingView + spreadsheet + analyst notes
versus
- one integrated decision workflow

Recommended page types:
1. CommodityNode vs TradingView for commodity-sensitive investors
2. CommodityNode vs generic finance news for commodity stock analysis
3. Bloomberg alternative for self-directed investors focused on commodity exposure
4. Best tools for tracking commodity impact on stocks

These should be honest comparison pages.

Positioning edge to emphasize:
- not just charts
- not just headlines
- not just research archive
- the workflow that translates commodity moves into equity decisions

## 9. Customer Research Plan (using customer-research)

The next level of marketing quality depends on VOC.

Research objectives:
1. learn how real users describe the “aha” moment
2. find the exact phrases they use for the problem
3. identify which workflow step sells the product
4. identify what makes users hesitate to buy

Best sources to analyze first:
- signup / conversion chats
- user replies / feedback / Telegram notes if available
- support emails
- comments from current readers
- Reddit / finance communities discussing commodity-linked stocks
- competitor reviews for adjacent products

Questions to answer:
- What do users think CommodityNode is replacing?
- Which part do they value most: reports, hub, simulator, or forecasts?
- Do they buy because of speed, clarity, confidence, or breadth?
- What wording resonates more: “commodity intelligence” or “stock impact” or “portfolio risk”?

## 12. Final Strategic Recommendation

Do not market CommodityNode as a “better commodity website.”
That is too small and too easy to misread.

Market it as:
- the decision workflow for commodity-sensitive investors and analysts
- the translation layer between commodity price action and equity positioning
- the place to go before the market fully reprices the downstream impact

That is the clearest strategic wedge.
