      <h2>1. Start With the Commodity Shock</h2>
      <p>Each report begins with a commodity move or scenario: a price surge, crash, regime shift, or thematic structural trend. We define the shock clearly so every downstream estimate has a consistent reference frame.</p>

      <h2>2. Map First-Order Exposure</h2>
      <p>We identify direct beneficiaries and losers first: producers, processors, heavy users, importers, exporters, and sector ETFs. These become the first ring in the impact map.</p>

      <h2>3. Extend Into Ripple Chains</h2>
      <p>Then we push outward into second-order and third-order effects — logistics, packaging, equipment suppliers, consumer pass-through, macro variables, and substitute materials. The goal is not to add noise, but to surface meaningful chain reactions.</p>

      <h2>4. Quantify Directional Sensitivity</h2>
      <p>Percentages in CommodityNode are research signals, not promises. They summarize directional sensitivity using historical relationships, business-model dependence, margin structure, and thematic exposure. These numbers are meant to accelerate deeper diligence.</p>

      <h2>5. Keep the Graph Readable</h2>
      <p>Impact maps prioritize interpretability over raw node count. We prefer a layered graph with controllable depth, clear labels, and visible relationship logic rather than a cluttered cloud of barely readable entities.</p>

      <h2>6. Treat Each Report as a Hub</h2>
      <p>Every report should connect outward to related tags, companies, sectors, and commodity hubs. Over time, the site becomes less like a post archive and more like an explorable commodity intelligence graph.</p>

      <h2>Important Caveat</h2>
      <p>CommodityNode is informational research, not personalized investment advice. Always validate assumptions with primary filings, company disclosures, and current market conditions before making decisions.</p>
