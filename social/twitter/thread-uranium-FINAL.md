**[댓글 3]**

Now the supply side. This is where it gets ugly.

Global uranium demand: ~180M lbs/year.
Global mine production: ~140M lbs/year.
Deficit: ~40M lbs/year.

This deficit has persisted for 5+ years. It's been filled by drawdowns from:
- Government stockpiles (running out)
- Underfeeding (maxed out)
- Secondary supplies (depleting fast)

---

**[댓글 4]**

Kazakhstan produces 43% of the world's uranium.

Kazatomprom just announced ANOTHER production miss — 10% below target for 2025 due to sulfuric acid shortages and construction delays.

The world's largest producer can't hit its own targets. Let that sink in.

---

**[댓글 5]**

Here's the geopolitical kicker:

Russia enriches 44% of the world's uranium fuel.
The US just banned Russian enriched uranium imports (HALEU ban effective 2024).

But the US has ZERO commercial enrichment capacity for HALEU (high-assay low-enriched uranium) — the fuel needed for next-gen SMRs.

The supply chain is broken.

---

**[댓글 6]**

The tariff war makes this worse.

US has no domestic uranium supply chain to speak of:
- Mining: <1% of global production
- Conversion: 1 facility (Honeywell, Metropolis IL)
- Enrichment: 1 facility (Urenco, Eunice NM — European-owned)

145% tariffs on Chinese goods → China controls rare earths for reactor components.

Energy independence? Not without uranium.

---

**[댓글 7]**

Spot uranium: ~$85/lb.
Long-term contract price: ~$80/lb.

Incentive price for new mine development: $75-90/lb (we're barely there).

But here's the thing — even at $100/lb, it takes 10-15 years to develop a new mine.

The Cigar Lake mine in Canada took 17 years from discovery to production.

There is no quick fix.

**[댓글 8]**

The decarbonization angle is the long-term structural driver.

28 countries signed the COP28 pledge to TRIPLE nuclear capacity by 2050.

That means going from 440 GW → 1,320 GW.

At current reactor sizes, that's ~880 new reactors.
Each needs initial fuel loading + annual reload = MASSIVE new uranium demand.
