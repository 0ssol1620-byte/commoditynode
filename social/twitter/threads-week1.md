**Tweet 3/10**

• Average time from discovery to production: 16 years

**Tweet 7/10**

**Tweet 8/10**

## Thread 2: Gold at $4,500

**Tweet 1/10**
🧵 Gold just hit $4,500.

Everyone's asking: is this a new floor or a blow-off top?

After analyzing central bank flows, real rates, and historical parallels — here's our take. ↓

**Tweet 2/10**
Central banks bought 1,037 tonnes in 2023. Then 1,045 tonnes in 2024. 2025 is tracking even higher.

This isn't a trade. It's a structural shift. Countries are de-dollarizing their reserves, and gold is the beneficiary.

**Tweet 3/10**
China's PBOC has added gold for 18 consecutive months. But here's the kicker: gold is still only ~5% of China's reserves vs. 70%+ for the US.

If China moves to even 10%, that's 2,000+ additional tonnes of demand. Annual mine supply globally? ~3,600 tonnes.

**Tweet 4/10**
Real yields tell the story. When US 10-year real yields were 2%+, gold "should have" dropped. It didn't.

Gold has decoupled from its traditional inverse correlation with real rates. That's new. And it suggests a regime change.

**Tweet 5/10**
The geopolitical premium is real:

• US-China tensions escalating
• Sanctions weaponization (Russia's $300B frozen reserves)
• BRICS exploring gold-backed trade settlement
• Middle East instability

Gold is becoming geopolitical insurance, not just an inflation hedge.

**Tweet 6/10**
ETF flows are finally catching up. After 3 years of outflows, gold ETFs saw $8.2B of inflows in Q1 2026.

Western investors are late to the party. Central banks and Asian retail were the first movers. When Western institutions pile in, that's when things get vertical.

**Tweet 7/10**
Historical blow-off tops look different:

1980: Gold spiked 120% in 4 months → crashed 65%
2011: Gold rose 45% in 6 months → fell 45% over 4 years

Current move: Gold up ~35% over 12 months. Steady, not parabolic. That's more "new regime" than "blow-off."

**Tweet 8/10**
The bear case:

• If the Fed cuts aggressively, risk-on could divert flows
• A sudden US-China détente removes geopolitical premium
• Physical demand from India/China slows on high prices
• Mining supply response (though this takes 7–10 years)

**Tweet 9/10**
Our view: $4,500 is more likely a new floor than a top.

The structural drivers — de-dollarization, central bank buying, geopolitical hedging — are multi-year trends, not trades.

Pullbacks to $4,000–4,200 are buying opportunities.

## Thread 4: Lithium's $6,000 Collapse

**Tweet 1/10**
🧵 Lithium went from $80,000/tonne to $6,000.

A 92% crash. The worst commodity bust in a decade.

But here's the paradox: EV sales hit all-time highs. Battery demand is soaring. So what happened? ↓

**Tweet 2/10**
The story starts in 2021–2022. EV demand exploded. Lithium couldn't keep up. Prices went parabolic — from $10,000 to $80,000/tonne.

Every miner on earth rushed to expand. Australia, Chile, Argentina, China — everyone was drilling.

**Tweet 3/10**
The supply response was massive:

• Australian spodumene production up 40% YoY
• African projects (Zimbabwe, DRC) came online faster than expected
• Chinese lepidolite processing expanded aggressively
• Total supply grew ~30% in 2 years

**Tweet 4/10**
Meanwhile, demand growth slowed — not in absolute terms, but vs. expectations.

China's EV penetration hit 40%+, meaning the growth RATE is decelerating even as volumes rise.

The lithium market went from deficit to surplus almost overnight.

**Tweet 5/10**
At $6,000/tonne, ~40% of global lithium production is below cash cost.

Mines are shutting down. Australian juniors are going bankrupt. Expansion projects are being shelved or cancelled.

This is the classic commodity cycle — but on fast-forward.

**Tweet 6/10**
The paradox: EV battery demand is projected to grow 25% annually through 2030. We NEED more lithium.

But at $6,000, no one can afford to produce it. And the mines being shut today take 3–5 years to restart.

We're setting up the NEXT shortage.

**Tweet 7/10**

## Thread 5: Uranium's Quiet Supply Crisis

**Tweet 1/10**
🧵 40 new nuclear reactors are under construction worldwide.

Meanwhile, uranium mines have been underinvesting for 15 years.

This is the quietest supply crisis in commodities. And it's about to get loud. ↓

**Tweet 2/10**
The numbers are stark:

• Global uranium demand: ~180 million lbs/year
• Global mine production: ~140 million lbs/year
• Deficit: ~40 million lbs/year
• Being filled by: secondary supplies (stockpiles, downblending) that are RUNNING OUT

**Tweet 3/10**
After Fukushima (2011), uranium crashed from $70 to $18/lb. Mines shut down. Exploration stopped. For 15 years, nobody invested.

Now 40 reactors are under construction (China: 24, India: 8, others: 8). Each needs ~500,000 lbs/year. That's 20M lbs of NEW annual demand.

**Tweet 4/10**
Kazakhstan produces 45% of global uranium (through Kazatomprom).

In 2024, they announced production would be 10–15% below targets due to sulfuric acid shortages and construction delays. In 2025, they cut guidance again.

The world's largest producer can't meet its own forecasts.

**Tweet 5/10**
The inventory buffer is shrinking fast:

• US utility inventories: ~2.5 years of forward coverage (vs. 4+ years historically)
• Japanese restarts pulling from spot market
• Conversion and enrichment bottlenecks creating additional constraints

You can mine uranium but still can't fuel a reactor without processing capacity.

**Tweet 6/10**
Policy tailwinds are enormous:

• US: bipartisan support for nuclear, $6B+ in subsidies
