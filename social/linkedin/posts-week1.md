## Post 1: Copper's AI Moment — Data Centers Are Driving a Copper Super-Cycle

The AI narrative has created trillion-dollar valuations for semiconductor companies. But almost no one is discussing the physical bottleneck that could constrain the entire buildout: copper.

Every hyperscale data center requires 20,000–40,000 tonnes of copper for power distribution, cooling systems, busbars, and cabling. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have collectively announced over 300 new data center projects through 2028. The copper demand from AI infrastructure alone could reach 6–12 million tonnes — against a global annual mine supply of just 22 million tonnes.

This comes on top of an already strained supply picture. The electrification of transport and energy systems is fundamentally copper-intensive, and the mining industry has chronically underinvested in new discoveries.

Here are five data points that frame the copper thesis:

• **Ore grade decline:** Average copper ore grades have fallen 25% over the past decade, meaning miners must process significantly more rock per tonne of copper produced
• **Development timelines:** The average time from copper deposit discovery to first production is 16 years — meaning supply responses to today's demand won't arrive until the late 2030s
• **Chile's constraints:** The world's largest copper producer (28% of global supply) faces water scarcity, rising costs, and regulatory uncertainty
• **Projected deficit:** The International Copper Study Group forecasts a 500,000+ tonne deficit in 2026, with some analysts projecting 4–6 million tonnes by 2030
• **Corporate signaling:** BHP's $49 billion bid for Anglo American in 2024 was primarily motivated by copper assets — the largest mining M&A signal in a decade

The copper super-cycle thesis isn't based on speculation. It's based on the intersection of two structural megatrends (AI and electrification) meeting a supply base that physically cannot scale fast enough.

Goldman Sachs is targeting $12,000/tonne. Current price sits around $9,500. Whether that target is hit in 2026 or 2028, the direction seems clear.

## Post 2: Gold at $4,500 — Regime Change or Blow-Off Top?

Gold crossing $4,500/oz has sparked intense debate. After a 35%+ rally over 12 months, the natural question is whether this is the beginning of a new price regime or the final stage of a speculative blow-off.

Having analyzed the flow data, macro backdrop, and historical parallels, we believe the evidence strongly favors "regime change" over "blow-off top."

The most important structural shift is central bank behavior. Central banks purchased over 1,000 tonnes of gold in each of 2023, 2024, and are on track to exceed that in 2025. This represents a fundamental reallocation of sovereign reserves away from dollar-denominated assets and toward physical gold. It is not a cyclical trade — it is a strategic repositioning driven by the weaponization of financial sanctions.

Five key factors supporting the new-floor thesis:

• **Central bank buying:** 1,045 tonnes purchased in 2024 alone. China's PBOC has added gold for 18 consecutive months, yet gold remains only ~5% of China's reserves versus 70%+ for the US
• **Decoupling from real yields:** Gold's 40-year inverse correlation with US real interest rates broke in 2023. Even with real yields above 2%, gold rallied — suggesting new demand drivers have overwhelmed traditional macro relationships
• **Geopolitical premium:** The freezing of Russia's $300B in reserves taught every sovereign wealth manager that reserves held in foreign systems carry confiscation risk. Gold is the only reserve asset with no counterparty risk
• **ETF flow inflection:** After three years of outflows, gold ETFs saw $8.2B of net inflows in Q1 2026. Western institutional investors are finally joining the trade that central banks and Asian retail started years ago
• **BRICS momentum:** Multiple BRICS nations are exploring gold-backed trade settlement mechanisms, which could create a structural new source of demand

The bear case — aggressive Fed cuts driving risk-on rotation, or a sudden geopolitical détente — is real but faces a high bar. De-dollarization and reserve diversification are multi-year, possibly multi-decade trends.

Historical blow-off tops (1980, 2011) featured parabolic moves of 45–120% in 4–6 months. The current move is 35% over 12 months — steady accumulation, not panic buying.

## Post 3: Black Sea Wheat — Is 2026 Setting Up a Repeat of 2022?

The global wheat market is showing warning signals that are uncomfortably similar to early 2022 — but with less buffer to absorb a shock.

In March 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine spiked wheat prices from $7.50 to $13.60/bushel within weeks. The world scrambled for alternatives, the UN brokered a grain corridor, and prices eventually normalized. But the structural damage to Black Sea production never fully healed.

Today, the setup may actually be more dangerous than 2022. Here's why:

• **Ukraine's diminished capacity:** Planted wheat area remains 20–25% below pre-war levels. Fields are mined, irrigation infrastructure is destroyed, and export logistics remain fragile and dependent on continued corridor access
• **Russian production risk:** Southern Russia is experiencing its worst drought in 15 years. The world's #1 wheat exporter is simultaneously weaponizing grain exports through informal quotas and variable export taxes
• **Global weather stress:** Australia faces El Niño-driven dryness. EU yields are projected 8–12% below five-year averages. Multiple major exporters are under pressure simultaneously
• **Depleted buffers:** Global wheat stocks-to-use ratio stands at 27% — the lowest since the 2007/08 food crisis. In 2022, the ratio was above 30%. There is less room to absorb a shock
• **Market complacency:** Speculative positioning is net short wheat. The market is priced for normalcy, not disruption

The humanitarian dimension makes this more than a trading story. Over 800 million people depend on Black Sea wheat. Egypt imports 12+ million tonnes annually. Food price spikes in import-dependent nations carry real risks of social instability — the Arab Spring was partly catalyzed by grain prices.

The 2022 playbook — spike, corridor deal, normalization — may not work again if the production shortfall is weather-driven rather than purely logistical.

## Post 4: Lithium's $6,000 Collapse — The EV Battery Paradox

Lithium carbonate has fallen from $80,000/tonne to approximately $6,000 — a 92% decline that represents one of the most dramatic commodity busts in recent history.

The paradox: this collapse happened while global EV sales hit record highs. Battery demand has never been stronger. So what explains the disconnect?

The answer is a classic commodity cycle compressed into fast-forward. The 2021–2022 price spike incentivized a massive supply response. Australian spodumene production surged 40% year-over-year. African projects accelerated. Chinese lepidolite processing expanded aggressively. Total supply grew approximately 30% in two years.

Simultaneously, demand growth decelerated — not in absolute terms, but relative to the exponential forecasts that justified $80,000 lithium. China's EV penetration exceeding 40% meant the growth rate was naturally slowing even as volumes continued rising.

Five critical observations on the lithium market today:

• **Below-cost production:** At $6,000/tonne, approximately 40% of global lithium production operates below cash cost. Mine closures and project cancellations are accelerating across Australia and South America
• **Supply destruction is future shortage:** The mines being shut today require 3–5 years to restart. Greenfield projects being shelved take even longer. Today's surplus is mechanically creating tomorrow's deficit
• **Demand trajectory unchanged:** EV battery demand is still projected to grow 25% annually through 2030. The structural need for lithium hasn't diminished — only the price signal to produce it has
• **Western supply chain paradox:** US IRA and EU Critical Raw Materials Act projects require $15,000–20,000/tonne lithium to be economically viable. At $6,000, these projects are unfinanceable, ensuring continued Chinese supply chain dominance
• **Historical precedent:** Uranium crashed 85% post-Fukushima, then entered a supply crisis. Cobalt crashed 70% in 2018, then tripled. Lithium itself crashed 50% in 2019 before rallying 700%. Busts create booms

The floor is likely in the $8,000–10,000 range based on marginal cost of production analysis. The timing of recovery depends on the pace of supply curtailment and whether Chinese producers maintain market-share-over-margins strategy.

## Post 5: Uranium's Quiet Supply Crisis — 40 Reactors, 15 Years of Underinvestment

There is arguably no commodity with a more compelling supply-demand imbalance than uranium. Yet it receives a fraction of the attention given to lithium, copper, or gold.

The numbers tell a straightforward story: global uranium demand runs approximately 180 million pounds per year. Mine production delivers roughly 140 million pounds. The 40-million-pound annual deficit has been filled by secondary supplies — government stockpiles, weapons downblending, and inventory drawdowns — that are measurably depleting.

Meanwhile, 40 new nuclear reactors are under construction worldwide, each requiring approximately 500,000 pounds of uranium annually. That represents 20 million pounds of entirely new annual demand being added to an already-deficit market.

Five factors that make uranium's supply crisis structural, not cyclical:

• **15 years of underinvestment:** After Fukushima crashed prices from $70 to $18/lb, exploration and mine development essentially stopped. New mine projects take 10–15 years from discovery to production. The investment gap cannot be closed quickly
• **Kazakhstan's production shortfalls:** Kazatomprom, responsible for 45% of global production, has missed production targets for two consecutive years due to sulfuric acid shortages and construction delays. The world's swing producer is struggling to maintain output, let alone grow it
• **Utility inventory drawdown:** US utilities have drawn inventories to approximately 2.5 years of forward coverage, versus 4+ years historically. Japanese reactor restarts are pulling from the spot market. Conversion and enrichment bottlenecks create additional supply chain constraints
• **Policy tailwinds are unprecedented:** The US passed bipartisan nuclear support legislation with $6B+ in subsidies. The EU classified nuclear as green taxonomy. China targets 150 GW by 2035 (up from 57 GW). Over 70 SMR designs are in development globally
• **Financial demand:** Sprott Physical Uranium Trust holds 66M+ pounds of physical uranium, effectively removing supply from the market. This "ETF effect" in a market 100x smaller than gold creates disproportionate price impact

Spot uranium has moved from $50 to $90+/lb. Long-term contract prices have risen to $75–80/lb, and utilities that delayed contracting are now competing for limited supply.

The nuclear renaissance is not a narrative — it is a policy consensus backed by construction activity. And the fuel supply chain has not invested enough to support it.
