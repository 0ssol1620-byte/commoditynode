Ammonia is the invisible backbone of global agriculture. Roughly 80% of all ammonia produced goes into nitrogen fertilizers — the single most important input for corn, wheat, and rice yields worldwide. Yet ammonia rarely makes headlines until its price spikes enough to threaten food security. In April 2026, we're approaching one of those inflection points.

## The Natural Gas Problem

Ammonia production is essentially a natural gas conversion process. The Haber-Bosch process — unchanged in principle for over a century — consumes approximately 33 million BTUs of natural gas per tonne of ammonia produced. This means natural gas represents **70-80% of ammonia's variable production cost**.

European natural gas prices remain elevated compared to pre-2022 norms, even as the acute energy crisis has faded. TTF benchmark prices have stabilized but remain roughly double the levels seen in 2019-2020. For European ammonia producers like Yara International and BASF, this means structurally higher production costs that squeeze margins whenever ammonia selling prices soften.

In the US, the picture is somewhat better. Henry Hub natural gas prices have remained comparatively subdued, giving American producers like CF Industries and LSB Industries a significant cost advantage. This US-Europe cost spread has persisted for nearly four years and shows no signs of narrowing.

## Global Supply Dynamics

The ammonia supply landscape is shifting. Several trends are converging in early 2026:

**Middle East capacity expansion:** Saudi Arabia and Qatar continue to bring new ammonia capacity online, leveraging their ultra-low natural gas costs. Ma'aden and SABIC joint ventures are expanding output, adding competitive pressure to global trade flows.

**Russia's ongoing role:** Despite Western sanctions and trade restrictions following the Ukraine conflict, Russia remains a major ammonia exporter. The Togliatti-Odessa pipeline — once a critical artery for Russian ammonia exports — remains largely offline, but Russian producers have rerouted volumes through Baltic and Far East ports. The net effect is higher logistics costs but continued supply presence in global markets.

**China's export restrictions:** China has periodically restricted fertilizer exports since 2021 to protect domestic agricultural supply. In early 2026, these restrictions remain partially in place, tightening the seaborne ammonia market and supporting prices in Southeast Asia.

## Demand Side: Planting Season Pressure

April marks the beginning of the Northern Hemisphere spring planting season — the peak demand period for nitrogen fertilizers. Farmers in the US Corn Belt, European grain regions, and South Asian rice paddies all draw heavily on ammonia-derived fertilizers during this window.

The demand outlook for 2026 is moderately strong. Global grain stocks remain below the five-year average for corn and wheat, incentivizing maximum planted acreage. The USDA's March Prospective Plantings report indicated US corn acreage intentions remain robust, which translates directly to urea and ammonia demand.

However, farmer purchasing behavior has become more cautious since the 2022 fertilizer price spike. Many operations now practice "just-in-time" purchasing rather than pre-season stockpiling, which creates sharper seasonal demand peaks but also means prices can whipsaw on short-term logistics disruptions.

## The Green Ammonia Wild Card

Green ammonia — produced using electrolysis-powered hydrogen instead of natural gas — remains a marginal contributor to total supply but is attracting outsized investment attention. Projects in Australia, Chile, and the Middle East aim to produce ammonia from renewable energy for both fertilizer use and as a potential shipping fuel.

In practice, green ammonia costs remain roughly 2-3x conventional production. No green ammonia project has yet reached commercial-scale economics competitive with Haber-Bosch. The long-term potential is genuine, but the short-term impact on ammonia markets is negligible.

## Key Risk Factors

- **Natural gas price volatility:** Any resurgence in European gas prices — whether from weather events, LNG supply disruptions, or geopolitical escalation — would immediately pressure ammonia margins
- **Trade policy shifts:** US tariff posture under the current administration could affect ammonia and fertilizer imports, particularly from Trinidad & Tobago (a major US supplier)
- **Weather-driven demand:** A late frost or drought in the US Corn Belt could reduce fertilizer application rates, softening demand during the critical Q2 window

## What to Watch

1. **Henry Hub and TTF natural gas prices through May** — the primary cost driver for ammonia economics
2. **USDA Planting Progress reports (weekly through June)** — corn planting pace directly correlates with nitrogen fertilizer pull-through
3. **China fertilizer export policy updates** — any tightening or loosening of export restrictions moves the seaborne ammonia market

## Signal Summary

Ammonia enters Q2 2026 in a state of tension between elevated input costs and moderate demand support from the planting season. The market is not in crisis, but margins are thin for high-cost producers, and any disruption to natural gas supply or trade flows could quickly tighten the market. For commodity watchers, ammonia is a leading indicator of food price inflation — and right now, the signal is cautiously neutral.
