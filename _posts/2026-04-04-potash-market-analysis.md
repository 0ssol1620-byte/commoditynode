Potash — potassium chloride (KCl) — is one of the three essential macronutrients for crop growth, alongside nitrogen and phosphorus. Without adequate potassium, plants cannot regulate water, resist disease, or produce robust yields. The global potash market, valued at roughly $30 billion annually, has been through an extraordinary period of disruption since 2022 — and in April 2026, the aftershocks are still reshaping trade flows.

## The Sanctions Legacy

The potash market was fundamentally altered by Western sanctions on Belarus (2021) and Russia (2022). Together, these two countries historically controlled approximately 40% of global potash exports. Belaruskali — the Belarusian state potash company — was directly sanctioned, while Russian producer Uralkali faced banking and logistics restrictions.

The immediate impact was dramatic. Potash prices tripled in 2022, from roughly $250/tonne to over $700/tonne, before gradually retreating as the market adapted. By early 2026, prices have settled into a range well above pre-sanctions levels but significantly below the 2022 spike.

**What's changed in the trade map:**
- Belarusian potash has been rerouted through Russian ports and Chinese rail, maintaining volume but at higher logistics cost
- Russian potash continues to reach global markets — India, China, Brazil, and Southeast Asia have not implemented potash-specific sanctions
- Canada's Nutrien and Mosaic have expanded production to fill Western market gaps
- New capacity in Laos (from Chinese-backed projects) and Eritrea (Colluli project) is beginning to contribute marginal supply

The net effect: the global potash market is no longer in crisis, but it operates with higher structural costs, less supply elasticity, and more geopolitical risk embedded in pricing than in the pre-2022 era.

## Demand: Spring Application Season

Potash demand follows agricultural cycles. In the Northern Hemisphere, spring application for corn, soybeans, and wheat drives the annual demand peak. In Brazil — the world's largest potash importer — demand peaks during the safrinha (second crop) corn planting season in February-March.

For the 2026 spring season, several demand factors are in play:

- **US farm economics:** Corn and soybean prices are moderate but not depressed, providing adequate farmer economics for full fertilizer application. The USDA's latest cost-of-production estimates show potash remaining affordable relative to crop revenue.
- **Brazil's import appetite:** Brazil imported record potash volumes in 2025 and is on pace for similar volumes in 2026. The country's dependence on imported potash (over 95% of consumption) makes it a price-sensitive but inelastic buyer.
- **India's subsidy framework:** India's nutrient-based subsidy (NBS) scheme determines the effective price Indian farmers pay for potash. The government has maintained subsidies at levels that support application rates, though there is perpetual uncertainty about subsidy renewal timing.

## The Canadian Expansion Play

Canada holds the world's largest potash reserves, primarily in Saskatchewan. Nutrien (formed from the PotashCorp-Agrium merger) and Mosaic are the dominant producers. Both companies have been ramping operational capacity:

- Nutrien has increased nameplate capacity and is running near operational maximums
- BHP's long-delayed Jansen potash project in Saskatchewan is progressing toward first production, expected in 2026-2027 — this will be the first new large-scale greenfield potash mine in decades

The Jansen project alone is expected to add 4.35 million tonnes of annual capacity at full build-out, representing roughly 6% of current global production. Its arrival will be the most significant supply-side event in potash markets in years.

## Key Risk Factors

- **Sanctions escalation or easing:** Any change in the Belarus/Russia sanctions framework directly impacts global potash supply costs and trade routes
- **BHP Jansen ramp-up delays:** Technical or regulatory delays to the Jansen project would tighten the medium-term supply outlook
- **Currency dynamics:** A strong US dollar raises the effective cost for importing nations (India, Brazil, Southeast Asia), potentially dampening demand

## What to Watch

1. **BHP Jansen project commissioning updates** — the single largest source of new potash supply globally; any timeline shift moves the market
2. **India's NBS subsidy renewal (typically announced mid-year)** — determines Indian potash purchasing pace for H2 2026
3. **Brazilian potash import volumes (MDIC trade data)** — leading indicator of Southern Hemisphere demand strength

## Signal Summary

Potash in April 2026 has found a fragile equilibrium. Sanctions have permanently altered trade flows without permanently constraining supply. Demand remains underpinned by global food production needs. The medium-term outlook hinges on new capacity — particularly BHP's Jansen mine — arriving on schedule to provide supply relief. For now, the market is balanced but brittle, with geopolitical risk as the primary source of potential disruption.
